# THREADBOX Brand Guidelines
### Malaysian Streetwear, Told Through Place

---

## 1. Brand Strategy

### Mission
THREADBOX makes streetwear that turns local geography into wearable pride — starting with Malaysia, narrowing to state, then to the corner of the map most brands never bother printing on a shirt (a jetty, a mini-league football pitch, a hawker stall). The mission is to make "kampung" and "local" feel as premium and covetable as any imported hype brand.

### Positioning Statement
For young Malaysians who are proud of where they're from but tired of either (a) tourist-shop batik clichés or (b) shirts that could be from any streetwear brand in the world — THREADBOX is the label that treats your hometown like a hypebeast treats a sneaker drop: researched, referenced, and released in limited runs.

### Target Audience
- **Core:** 18–28, Malaysian, urban/suburban, streetwear-literate (follows drop culture, sneakers, football, local F&B scenes). Active on Instagram/TikTok/Threads.
- **Secondary:** Malaysians in their 30s–40s with nostalgia for a specific hometown, boarding school, or kampung — buy as identity/gift pieces.
- **Occasion-driven buyers:** People buying *into* a specific moment — Merdeka season, a local football tournament, a reunion — not just "a t-shirt brand."

### Tone of Voice
- **Proud, not preachy.** Patriotism expressed as design flex, never as a lecture.
- **Local slang, global execution.** Copy can drop BM words (Merdeka, Balik Pulau, Tanah Tumpah Darahku) unapologetically — no translation apology, no italics-as-foreign-word treatment.
- **Understated confidence.** Let the illustration do the shouting; captions and product copy stay short, dry, a little wry.
- **Community-first for collabs.** When a design is tied to a real event (Balik Pulau mini cup), the tone shifts to "we made this *for* you, not *at* you" — credit the academies, the place, the people.

### Brand Values
1. **Specificity over symbolism.** A clock tower silhouette says more than a generic hibiscus-and-flag mashup. Always earn the reference.
2. **Limited by design.** Small runs (50–150 units) per SKU — scarcity is structural, not a marketing gimmick.
3. **Place is the product line.** The brand's expansion logic *is* the geography of Malaysia. This isn't a tagline, it's the org chart for design (see Section 4).
4. **Craft in the details.** Puff print on the wordmark, tonal stitching, tags that reference the collection — small print-production choices that reward close inspection.

## 2. Creative Direction

### Visual Principles
1. **Two lanes, one wordmark.** Every SKU carries the THREADBOX chest logo on front — small, quiet, never competing with the back print. The *back* is where the story lives.
2. **Front is restrained, back is the poster.** Front placement is a single-color or two-color lockup, chest-left-of-center or dead-center, no larger than ~10cm wide. The back graphic is the hero — full upper-back real estate, always with a title lockup underneath (place name, event name, or theme word).
3. **Illustration earns its place.** Every recurring motif (clock tower, jetty, hibiscus, moon+star, food item) must correspond to something real about the place being depicted — no stock "Asian aesthetic" filler.
4. **Color tells you which collection you're in** before you read the words. See color system below — a viewer should be able to sort these three drops by palette alone.
5. **One aesthetic risk per drop, not per shirt.** Don't chase five illustration styles inside one collection. Pick the register (vintage travel poster / manga collage / editorial type) per collection and hold the line across all SKUs in that drop.

### Illustration Style — Three Registers (in current use)
Based on the drafts, THREADBOX is actually already running three distinct illustration languages. Name them, keep them intentional, and don't let them blur:

**A. Vintage Travel-Poster Register** (Merdeka drop, Penang City drop)
- Mid-century travel-poster / propaganda-poster illustration: flat color blocking, halftone or grain texture, dramatic low-angle architecture (clock towers, colonial shophouses), warm restricted palettes.
- Reference feel: 1950s tourism board poster, not a Canva "Malaysia clipart" pack.
- Used for: Merdeka nationalism-themed pieces, Penang City heritage piece.

**B. Bold Sans Typographic Register** (Penang wordmark piece, Balik Pulau typographic piece)
- Oversized, stacked, condensed sans-serif type *is* the graphic — letterforms sometimes contain or interweave small illustrated icons (Penang's skyline sitting inside the letters).
- No illustration needed if the type carries enough weight and craft.
- Used for: statement/wordmark pieces that need to work even at a glance from across a room (event tees, hype-drop tees).

**C. Manga/Collage Sports Register** (Balik Pulau football-collab drop)
- Dense collage composition, manga-influenced figure inking, palm fronds and local flora woven between player figures, trophy, road-sign call-out ("BALIK PULAU" as a literal street sign).
- High detail density is intentional here — it mirrors sports-manga poster culture (Captain Tsubasa-adjacent), which is exactly the reference point for a youth football tournament.
- Used for: the football-cup collab only. This register should stay reserved for sport/competition collabs so it doesn't dilute into a generic "manga tee" brand.

**Rule:** never mix registers on the same shirt. One register per SKU, chosen for what the content demands — not for variety's sake.

### Typography

**Wordmark:** THREADBOX logotype — bold, condensed, geometric sans. The middle "E" is replaced with a speed-line/stripe glyph (as seen in current drafts) — this is the one non-negotiable brand asset. Keep it monochrome on garment (white on black tee, black on white tee); never recolor it to match a collection palette. It's the anchor that says "same brand" across wildly different back-print styles.

**Display / Headline (back-print titles — "MERDEKA," "PENANG," "BALIK PULAU"):**
- Condensed bold sans or hand-lettered script depending on register (script for Merdeka's painterly mark, blocky condensed sans for Penang/Balik Pulau).
- Recommend standardizing on a single condensed grotesk family for all *typographic-register* headlines (e.g., a family like Druk, Anton, or a similarly heavy condensed sans) so future drops feel related even when illustration style changes. Reserve script/brush lettering as an occasional accent, not a second default.

**Body / Web / Product Copy:**
- A clean, neutral grotesk for anything utilitarian (product descriptions, size charts, site nav) — this should *not* try to look "streetwear," it should get out of the way so the garment graphics stay the loud element.
- Avoid pairing THREADBOX's bold condensed identity with a soft rounded UI font on the website — it will read as generic SaaS, not apparel.

### Color System

**Base:** Every SKU lives on black or white cotton. No exceptions for now — it's a brand-recognition anchor.

**Per-collection accent palettes** (derived from current drafts — lock these in, refine hex on real proofs):

| Collection | Palette | Feel |
|---|---|---|
| **Malaysia / Merdeka** | Flag red `#CC1F2E`, gold `#F2C230`, navy `#1B2E5C`, cream `#F0E6D2`, off-white | National, warm, a little worn-in (grain/halftone, not flat corporate flag colors) |
| **Penang** | Teal/harbor blue `#1E7A8C`, cream `#F3E9D2`, warm red `#B8352E`, charcoal | Heritage port town — clay-tile roofs, sea, old shophouse paint |
| **Balik Pulau (football collab)** | Cobalt/navy `#16386B`, gold `#F0B429`, white | Team-kit energy — reads as sportswear, not streetwear, on purpose |

**Rule of thumb:** if a future drop is about *food*, warm terracotta/charcoal; about *festival*, brighter saturated pairs; about *nature/landmark*, the teal/cream heritage family. Palette should be decidable from the collection tier (see Section 4) before a single asset is drawn.

### Layout Rules
- **Front chest:** logo only, ~8–10cm wide, centered or left-chest. One color.
- **Back placement:** upper-back, centered, roughly shoulder-blade-to-mid-back. Title lockup sits directly under the illustration, not floating separately.
- **Hem/neck tag:** small woven tag with era/collection code (already in use — keep this, it's a nice "look closer" detail that rewards collectors).
- **Negative space is a feature.** Every draft shown leaves the lower back and sleeves untouched — preserve this; it's what keeps the pieces wearable rather than looking like a poster stapled to a shirt.

## 3. Graphic Design Bible

### Composition Templates
1. **Poster-Back Template** (Merdeka, Penang City): full-bleed rectangular or silhouette-shaped illustration + title wordmark centered below, optional tagline in small caps beneath the title.
2. **Stacked-Type Template** (Penang wordmark tee): city/place name broken across 3–4 stacked lines, giant scale, with small illustrated vignettes inhabiting the negative space of the letterforms.
3. **Collage-Badge Template** (Balik Pulau): dense central illustration inside an implied badge/crest shape, title treated as a literal signpost/street-sign graphic, small chest crest logo as the "team badge" front counterpart.
4. **Typographic Statement Template** (Shabab Super Cup tee): no illustration at all — three stacked bold words, one small icon accent, an eyebrow line above in small caps (event name). Reserve this for event/hype tees that need instant legibility.

### Recurring Motifs (earn these, don't decorate with them)
- Moon and star (Merdeka/national identity)
- Hibiscus (Bunga Raya — national flower, use sparingly, not as filler)
- Clock towers / colonial shophouse architecture (heritage, Penang & KL)
- Jetty / clan jetty structures (Penang-specific, don't reuse outside Penang drops)
- Local food items rendered as illustrated icons with BM/local names labeled (char koay teow, asam laksa) — a strong differentiator, keep expanding this per district
- Road/street signage graphic style for place names (Balik Pulau) — good device for district-level drops specifically, since districts often lack a single iconic landmark

### Print Techniques
- Standard screen print for multi-color poster backs (Merdeka, Penang City) — halftone dot textures reproduce well in screen print and reinforce the vintage-poster register.
- Puff print reserved for the wordmark only, sparingly, on premium/anniversary pieces — don't puff-print everything or it stops being special.
- Discharge/water-based ink recommended on black tees for the softer hand-feel — avoids the "plastic sticker" look that hurts a lot of small streetwear brands' garments.
- Woven neck labels for collection/era codes — already established, continue.

### Do's and Don'ts

**Do**
- Research one real, specific detail per design (an actual street name, an actual dish, an actual building) before drawing anything.
- Keep front logo monochrome and small regardless of collection.
- Let illustration density match the subject's actual density (a quiet fishing village jetty deserves negative space; a chaotic football tournament deserves collage).

**Don't**
- Don't put batik patterns, wau (kite), or songket motifs on a shirt unless the specific drop is actually about that craft — using them as generic "Malaysian texture" filler is the tourist-shop trap the brand exists to avoid.
- Don't recolor the wordmark to match collection palettes — it dilutes brand recognition.
- Don't mix illustration registers (A/B/C above) within a single drop.
- Don't scale collage-register illustration (Balik Pulau style) down to chest-size — it was designed for back-scale density and will look muddy small.

## 4. Collection Framework
### Malaysia → State → District → Landmark → Culture → Food → Festival

This is the literal expansion logic for SKUs, not just a metaphor. Each tier narrows scope and should shift register/palette predictably:

```
TIER 0 — MALAYSIA (national)
   → tone: patriotic, Merdeka-adjacent, broad symbols (flag, map, moon+star)
   → register: A (vintage poster) or type-led statement pieces
   → e.g. current "MALAYSIA / Tanah Tumpah Darahku" and Merdeka SKUs

TIER 1 — STATE (e.g. Penang, Selangor, Sabah)
   → tone: heritage + regional pride, state-level icons (skyline, state flower/food)
   → register: A (heritage poster) or B (stacked-type with skyline vignettes)
   → e.g. current "PENANG CITY" and "P E N A N G" stacked-type SKUs

TIER 2 — DISTRICT (e.g. Balik Pulau, George Town, Bukit Mertajam)
   → tone: hyper-local, insider references, community/event-driven
   → register: C (collage) for event collabs, or road-sign/typographic for standalone district drops
   → e.g. current "BALIK PULAU" football-collab SKUs

TIER 3 — LANDMARK (e.g. Kek Lok Si, Penang Hill, a specific jetty or clan house)
   → tone: single-subject reverence, most "illustration-forward" tier
   → register: A, treated almost like single-subject poster art

TIER 4 — CULTURE (e.g. Peranakan heritage, a specific craft, a dialect/phrase)
   → tone: educational-but-cool, needs the most research per Do's/Don'ts above
   → register: type-led, motif used only if tied to that specific culture

TIER 5 — FOOD (e.g. char koay teow, asam laksa, nasi kandar)
   → tone: playful, appetite-driven, good for smaller/cheaper accessory drops (caps, tote bags)
   → register: illustrated food icon + comic-panel or badge layout

TIER 6 — FESTIVAL (e.g. Chinese New Year in Penang, Thaipusam, CNY lion dance in George Town)
   → tone: celebratory, seasonal, time-boxed drops (good for urgency/scarcity marketing)
   → register: brighter saturated palettes, festival-specific motifs only
```

**How the current 3 SKUs map:**
| SKU | Tier | Register | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| Malaysia / Merdeka | Tier 0 | A / type-led | National-scale, Merdeka-season timing |
| Penang City / Penang wordmark | Tier 1 | A + B | State-level heritage, two treatments to A/B test which resonates |
| Balik Pulau (football collab) | Tier 2 | C | District-level, tied to a real community event |

**Expansion guardrail:** don't skip tiers. Going straight from Tier 0 (Malaysia) to Tier 3 (a single landmark) without ever doing Tier 1/2 work will read as random rather than a coherent "we're mapping the whole country" brand story. Sequence future drops so the *tier itself* becomes part of the brand narrative you can talk about publicly (e.g., "Tier 2: District" as a named drop-series on socials).
